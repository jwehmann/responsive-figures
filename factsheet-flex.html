Racial Gaps in Access to Opportunity by Transit in Birmingham-Hoover

Access to opportunities like jobs, grocery stores, and recreational activities is not evenly distributed across the US. A history of racist policymaking and planning has led to segregated neighborhoods that keep many people of color far from opportunity. It has also led to a mismatch between where jobs are located and where job seekers live, which can cause high unemployment rates and lead to longer spells of joblessness.

These fact sheets are meant to help community members and local leaders see where they rank within their metropolitan region in terms of gaps in access to opportunity and help them to target transportation, community development, and workforce training investments into the areas that have the largest gaps in access to opportunity. They can also look at how their gaps in access to opportunity have changed over time to see whether they are moving closer to an equitable community or further from it.

To increase access to opportunity for people of color, leaders in the Birmingham-Hoover region could increase equity in transportation through things such as mobility equity councils and robust community engagement efforts that ensure that transportation decisions are led by the needs of people of color. They could also increase equity within community and economic development by implementing flexible, long-term financing and boosting funding for community development capacity to absorb and more broadly distribute funding. And, they could restructure land uses to increase the supply of housing (especially affordable housing) near transit, and better coordinate land use, housing, transportation, and economic development to ensure that new opportunities are accessible to people of color.
